\title{Projecting NBA Rookie-Contract Value from College Statistics\\
\large A supervised and unsupervised pipeline over 1,042 draftees, 2000--2022}

\author{\IEEEauthorblockN{Colin Hanley}
\IEEEauthorblockA{\textit{CS 451 --- Introduction to Data Science}\\
Spring 2026}}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We ask how much of an NBA player's first four years of on-court value --
measured by cumulative Value Over Replacement Player (VORP) -- can be
recovered from their college box-score statistics and pre-college recruiting
profile alone. We assemble a dataset of 1{,}042 college players drafted
between 2000 and 2022, engineer 13 features beyond the 28 raw columns
(including position-relative z-scores, a volume-weighted three-point
percentage, and defensive-versatility interactions), and train three
regression models (Ridge, XGBoost, and an MLP) with a strict temporal
train/test split. XGBoost wins on every metric, with a hold-out
$R^2 = 0.202$, MAE of 1.67 VORP, and an AUC of 0.591 on the binarised
positive-VORP classification task. Unsupervised K-means fit within each
position group surfaces eight interpretable player archetypes (three guard
types, three forward types, two center types) whose mean future VORP
differs by a factor of ten. The trained pipeline is shipped as an
interactive dashboard that exposes Ridge coefficients to the browser for
live predictions and displays a projected 2026-class big board with SHAP
drivers and same-position historical comparables. Four-fifths of the
variance in four-year VORP remains unexplained by college-era features
alone -- a quantitative bound on how far statistical scouting can go
without scouting.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
sports analytics, gradient boosting, SHAP, K-means, feature engineering,

\section{Introduction}

Every June, NBA front offices assemble a public-facing ranked list of
roughly sixty college and international prospects and commit tens of
millions of dollars in contracts based on that ordering. Scouts watch
video, interview players, and argue about motor; the result is a draft
order whose quality is only apparent four years later, when rookie
contracts expire and retrospective value metrics become credible.

This project asks a narrower, more tractable question:
\emph{given only what a prospect did in college, and what he was thought
of in high school, how much of his four-year NBA value can a machine
recover?} The answer bounds the value a purely statistical scout can add
to a human-intensive process, and in doing so separates patterns the box
score already contains from patterns that require tape study, physical
measurements, or interviews to see.

We frame it as both a regression task (predicting cumulative four-year
VORP from a player's rookie contract) and a binary classification task
(predicting whether that four-year VORP is positive --- i.e.\ whether the
player ever contributed at a replacement-level-or-better rate). We
additionally use unsupervised clustering to discover interpretable
player archetypes without reference to outcomes, and ship the entire
pipeline as an interactive editorial dashboard that applies the model to
the incoming 2026 draft class.

\textbf{Contributions.} (i) An end-to-end pipeline from scraped
historical data through model comparison, explanation, and deployment;
(ii) a volume-weighted three-point-percentage feature that shrinks low-
attempt players toward the league mean, removing 0\,\%/100\,\% noise;
(iii) per-position K-means producing eight interpretable archetypes with
auto-derived labels that pass absolute basketball thresholds, not just
within-cluster z-scores; (iv) a deployable static dashboard whose Ridge
live predictor runs entirely in the browser with no server.

\section{Related Work}

Draft prediction has a long public-facing history in basketball
analytics. Oliver's \emph{Basketball on Paper}~\cite{oliver2004basketball}
formalised the possession-based efficiency metrics (offensive rating,
defensive rating, true shooting) that later underpin our feature set.
The NBA's retrospective Box Plus/Minus and VORP
frameworks~\cite{myers2020bpm} give us the target variable: VORP weights
a player's BPM by minutes played and baselines against a
replacement-level rotation player.

Gradient-boosted trees~\cite{chen2016xgboost} are the dominant family for
tabular prediction tasks of this size and structure; SHAP
values~\cite{lundberg2017shap} give us a principled, per-prediction
explanation we can surface in the dashboard without sacrificing the
predictive accuracy of a non-linear model. Both are used via their
canonical implementations; all scikit-learn
algorithms~\cite{pedregosa2011sklearn} are used off the shelf.

The most visible public draft-prediction tooling is ESPN's BPI Draft Model
and 538's CARMELO/DRAYMOND historical projections, both of which combine
college stats with physical measurements and scouting rank. Our work
\emph{differs} from these in three ways: (a) we use exclusively
free public data (Basketball-Reference~\cite{bbref} and the 247Sports
composite~\cite{247sports}), making the pipeline fully reproducible; (b)
our model is position-aware through engineered z-score features rather
than through position-specific model heads; and (c) our archetype
discovery is unsupervised --- labels are derived from within-cluster
stat deviations rather than hand-assigned.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Sources}
Two public sources were scraped:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Basketball-Reference}~\cite{bbref}: per-player college career
    totals, advanced metrics (WS, WS/40, TOV\%, FTr, three-point
    attempt rate), draft position, and first four seasons of NBA box-score
    and advanced metrics.
  \item \textbf{247Sports composite}~\cite{247sports}: high-school recruiting
    rank, coerced to an integer $1\ldots101$, with 101 used as a sentinel
    for unranked players.
\end{itemize}

The resulting dataset is 1{,}042 players drafted between 2000 and 2022.
One row was dropped for a missing NBA target (career-ending injury before
reaching the NBA), and two rows had obvious scraping-error
\texttt{fg\_pct} values (Hamady N'Diaye's 2.2 and Tristan Thompson's 30.7)
which we manually corrected using the companion \texttt{ts\_pct} field.

\subsection{Target}
Our primary regression target is \texttt{nba\_4yr\_vorp} -- the sum of a
player's Value Over Replacement Player statistic across their first four
NBA seasons. The classification target \texttt{PosVORP} is simply
\texttt{(nba\_4yr\_vorp $> 0$)}. Target distribution is right-skewed
(\cref{fig:target}): the median drafted player accumulates roughly 0.6
VORP over four years, while stars reach 20+.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{01_target_distribution}
  \caption{Distribution of the regression target (4-year VORP) and the
    complementary BPM target. Both are heavily right-skewed; roughly
    half of drafted players finish their rookie contract at
    negative VORP.}
  \label{fig:target}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Feature schema}
After cleaning we retain 28 raw columns: 10 per-game box-score stats,
8 percentage and advanced stats, 4 cumulative win-share columns, 2 physical
measurements, and 4 discrete indicators (position, college seasons,
one-and-done, recruit tier). To these we add 13 engineered features
(\cref{sec:methodology}). The final feature matrix has
$n = 1{,}042$ rows and 41 columns.

A correlation scan (\cref{fig:corr}) shows the strongest raw
correlates with four-year VORP are college \texttt{ws\_40} ($r\approx 0.29$)
and draft-pick number ($r\approx -0.40$ --- not a feature, shown for
reference). Individual box-score stats like PPG and RPG correlate only
weakly, because they mean different things at different positions.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{04_correlation_heatmap}
  \caption{Pearson correlation matrix for raw features and the
    regression target. The strongest positive correlate with NBA VORP
    among pre-engineering features is college WS/40.}
  \label{fig:corr}
\end{figure}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Feature engineering}
Raw per-game stats mean different things at different positions: 4\,RPG
is league-average for a guard and terrible for a center. We therefore
engineer \emph{position-relative z-scores} for seven stats
(PPG, RPG, APG, SPG, BPG, TS\%, WS/40) using per-position means and
standard deviations computed from the training set. These seven
\texttt{\_vs\_pos} columns turn out to dominate the SHAP importance
ranking downstream (\cref{sec:results}).

Three interaction features capture non-linear signal:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{def\_versatility} $= \text{SPG} \times \text{BPG}$,
    which combines perimeter and interior defense into a single scalar
    that rewards players who contribute to both.
  \item \texttt{recruit\_x\_ws40} $= (101 - \text{recruit\_rank})
    \cdot \text{WS/40}$, asking whether a player lived up to their
    high-school hype.
  \item \texttt{ws40\_per\_season} $= \text{WS/40} \div
    \text{college\_seasons}$, penalising players who needed four years
    to reach efficient production.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Volume-weighted three-point percentage.} Raw
\texttt{three\_pt\_pct} contains serious noise from low-attempt
players -- multiple centers post 0\,\% on one attempt, and several wings
post 100\,\% on two. We replace it with a shrinkage estimate toward the
league mean:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{p}_3 \;=\;
\frac{p_3 \cdot r_3 + \bar{p}_3 \cdot k}{r_3 + k}
\end{equation}
where $p_3$ is the raw 3P\%, $r_3$ is three-point attempt rate,
$\bar{p}_3 = 0.312$ is the dataset-wide mean, and $k = 0.083$ is the
25\textsuperscript{th} percentile of $r_3$ in the training set. Under
this transform, a player with 100\,\% on two attempts gets pulled almost
entirely to the league mean, while a genuine volume shooter at 40\,\%
with a 0.60 attempt rate barely shifts. This replaces
\texttt{three\_pt\_pct} in the feature matrix rather than augmenting it.

A final \texttt{build\_score} feature sums position-relative height and
weight z-scores, capturing physical outliers for each position.

\subsection{Train/test split}
We adopt a \emph{temporal} train/test split at draft class 2019: train on
the 851 players drafted 2000--2018, test on the 191 players drafted
2019--2022. Randomly shuffling would leak signal from later players
into the model through feature distribution changes (e.g.\ a steadily
rising three-point attempt rate). The temporal split also simulates the
real-world use case: a team in 2026 has four-year outcomes only for
players drafted through 2022.

\subsection{Models}
Three regressors are trained with identical 5-fold cross-validation
inside their respective hyperparameter searches:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ridge}~\cite{pedregosa2011sklearn} with $\alpha=10$ as an
    interpretable linear baseline. Its coefficients are exported to the
    browser for the dashboard's live predictor.
  \item \textbf{XGBoost}~\cite{chen2016xgboost} with a 50-iteration
    randomised hyperparameter search over \texttt{n\_estimators},
    \texttt{max\_depth}, \texttt{learning\_rate}, \texttt{subsample},
    \texttt{colsample\_bytree}, and \texttt{min\_child\_weight}.
  \item \textbf{Multi-layer perceptron} (scikit-learn MLP) with 20
    randomised search iterations over hidden layer shapes (two to four
    layers, 16--128 units), initial learning rate, and $L^2$
    regularisation.
\end{itemize}
Each regressor has a parallel classification head trained on
\texttt{PosVORP} with the same search budget. All features are scaled
with a \texttt{StandardScaler} fit only on the training set.

\subsection{Unsupervised archetypes}
We cluster the full (train + test) 1{,}042-row scaled feature matrix
with K-means, but separately within each position group:
$k = 3$ for guards (480 players), $k = 3$ for forwards (442), and
$k = 2$ for centers (120). A single global K-means mostly just
re-discovers the interior-vs-perimeter split (silhouette peaks at
$k=2$); per-position clustering yields homogeneous archetypes within
each role. Global PCA to two components (explaining $22.2 + 14.4 = 36.6\,\%$
of total variance) provides a shared 2D embedding for visualisation.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{11_kmeans_selection}
  \caption{Silhouette score by $k$, computed within each position group.
    Dashed vertical lines mark the chosen $k$. Guard and forward
    silhouettes are flat past $k=3$; centers are too small a group to
    support more than two stable clusters.}
  \label{fig:kselect}
\end{figure}

Archetype labels are derived client-side from each cluster's stat
profile. A label is applied if (a) the within-position z-score exceeds
a positive-z threshold and (b) the absolute stat value clears a
basketball-literate floor (e.g.\ ``Rim-protecting'' requires
$\text{BPG}\geq 1.0$, not just above-peer BPG). A
$1.3\times$-the-floor override handles the two-cluster edge case where
the second-best center still merits ``Rim-protecting'' despite
a negative within-group z. Clusters with no positive-z label earning
a floor are called ``Traditional \emph{$\langle$position$\rangle$}''.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Regression}
\cref{tab:regression} summarises hold-out performance.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{Regression on 4-year VORP, test set (2019--2022, $n=191$).}
  \label{tab:regression}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
    \toprule
    Model            & $R^2$  & MAE  & RMSE \\
    \midrule
    Ridge (baseline) & 0.162  & 1.78 & 2.35 \\
    \textbf{XGBoost} & \textbf{0.202} & \textbf{1.67} & \textbf{2.29} \\
    Neural Network   & 0.152  & 1.65 & 2.36 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

XGBoost wins on $R^2$ and RMSE; the MLP achieves the narrowest MAE,
suggesting its errors are more symmetric but its large misses are
larger. Ridge -- a one-line linear model -- is within 4\,$R^2$ points
of the winner, which both validates the feature engineering and hints
that we are near the ceiling of what the linear signal in these
features can deliver.

\subsection{Classification}
On the binarised positive-VORP classifier, XGBoost lands at 56.5\,\%
accuracy, 0.491 F1, and 0.591 AUC-ROC, narrowly ahead of the MLP
(55.5\,\% / 0.479 / 0.579). An AUC of 0.591 is modestly better than
chance, reflecting the same underlying truth as the regression $R^2$:
four-fifths of the outcome variance lies outside the features we have.

\subsection{Feature importance}
\cref{fig:shap} shows the mean $|\text{SHAP}|$ ranking over the test set.
Four of the top five features are \emph{position-relative} variants of
raw stats, confirming that raw box-score columns are dominated by their
z-score counterparts. Win Shares per 40 minutes (both position-relative
and absolute) dominates the top slot, followed by playmaking
(\texttt{apg\_vs\_pos}) and disruption (\texttt{spg}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{10_shap_importance}
  \caption{Mean absolute SHAP values on the test set. Position-relative
    z-score features occupy four of the top five slots, validating the
    engineering choice to normalise by position.}
  \label{fig:shap}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Archetypes and outcomes}
\cref{fig:archetypes} plots the PCA embedding coloured by cluster, along
with mean four-year VORP per cluster. The ``Scoring forward'' archetype
(cluster 5, $n=101$, 13.7\,PPG, 7.4\,RPG, 1.42\,BPG) has the highest mean
VORP at $+2.17$, while the ``Traditional center'' cluster (cluster 6,
$n=82$) sits near replacement level at $+0.17$. The factor-of-ten spread
across archetypes is much larger than the gap between the best and worst
model on the test set, suggesting that \emph{which} kind of player you
draft is a first-order signal -- one the unsupervised method found
without reference to NBA outcomes.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{12_kmeans_clusters}
  \caption{\textbf{Left:} per-position K-means clusters projected onto a
    shared two-dimensional PCA. Marker shape encodes position, colour
    encodes cluster. \textbf{Right:} mean four-year VORP by cluster, with
    per-cluster sample size.}
  \label{fig:archetypes}
\end{figure}

\subsection{2026 projections}
Applying the trained XGBoost regressor to the 2026 draft class yields
Cameron Boozer (Duke) at the top with a projected $+9.29$ four-year
VORP, followed by Caleb Wilson (North Carolina, $+5.56$), Kingston
Flemings (Houston, $+4.53$), and Allen Graves (Santa Clara, $+4.48$).
The Graves projection is instructive: his college stat line (0.613 TS\%,
1.9 SPG, 41\,\% on three-point volume, one-and-done) is genuinely
elite-for-his-age, yet conventional scouting does not have him near the
top of its boards. That gap is exactly the 80\,\% of variance the model
does not capture: strength of schedule, tape study, and physical
projection.

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{What worked.} Position-relative z-scoring was the single most
impactful feature-engineering decision, validated by SHAP: four of the
top five features are \texttt{\_vs\_pos} variants. The volume-weighted
three-point percentage modestly improved correlation with VORP
($r: 0.023 \to 0.033$) and, more importantly, stabilised the tails of
the feature distribution. Per-position K-means (over a single global
K-means) produced interpretable archetypes at the cost of a slightly
lower silhouette score -- a trade we are comfortable making because the
goal was human-readable archetypes, not maximum-compactness clusters.

\textbf{What didn't.} The neural network never matched XGBoost despite
comparable hyperparameter budget. With only 851 training rows this is
expected: deep learning's benefit over trees is a feature-interaction
exploitation that requires more data to compete with boosted trees'
greedy split search. We also tried dropping the volume-weighted 3P\%
feature entirely and the gap was within noise -- XGBoost is robust to
the exact functional form of engineered features.

\textbf{Limitations.} Our $R^2$ of 0.202 bounds the value of purely
statistical college-era scouting. The four-fifths of variance we don't
capture includes: (a) strength of schedule (a 20-PPG scorer in the SEC
$\neq$ a 20-PPG scorer in the Big South); (b) shot selection (a 50\,\%
FG shooter with 60\,\% at the rim is different from one with 40\,\% on
threes); (c) physical-measurement data (wingspan, reach, combine
testing) which are public but unevenly available pre-2014; and (d)
injury and developmental history. A future version of this work would
ingest at least (a) and (c).

Position labels are also noisy. ``Forward'' conflates wings and interior
players; the archetype clustering partially corrects for this (the
two interior-leaning forward clusters separate from the perimeter-leaning
one on PC2) but the position label itself remains a coarse feature.
Finally, era drift is unaddressed: a player drafted in 2003 plays a
different game than one drafted in 2022. We did not attempt era
normalisation.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

Given only college box scores and high-school recruiting rank, a
well-tuned XGBoost model explains 20\,\% of the variance in four-year
NBA VORP on a held-out 2019--2022 test set -- a modest but real signal,
and one that substantially outperforms a linear baseline only because of
carefully engineered position-relative features. Unsupervised clustering
within position groups discovers eight interpretable archetypes whose
NBA outcomes differ by an order of magnitude, suggesting that ``what
kind of prospect'' is a first-order signal even before ``how good.''

Future work should incorporate strength-of-schedule adjustments, combine
measurements, and possibly per-game shot-chart data now being made
publicly available by the NCAA. An ensemble with NBA-side scouting
priors (e.g.\ mock-draft consensus) could be tested for residual
predictive content beyond the college statistical signal. Finally, the
same pipeline can be retargeted to predict \emph{second-contract} value
rather than rookie-contract value, which would better capture long-term
NBA impact.

\section{Individual Contributions}

This is a solo project. All data acquisition, cleaning, feature
engineering, modelling, frontend implementation, and writing was done by
Colin Hanley.

\paragraph*{Personal reflection.}
The most valuable lesson was how much of the project's quality lived in
feature engineering and error analysis, not in model selection. The gap
between Ridge and XGBoost (4 $R^2$ points) was smaller than the gap
between raw features and position-relative features (which I did not
measure rigorously but was visible in SHAP). What I would do differently
next time: set up an ablation study early so the relative contribution
of each engineered feature is measured, not assumed; and collect
strength-of-schedule data from the start rather than leaving it as
future work.
