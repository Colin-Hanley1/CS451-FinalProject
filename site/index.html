FROM THE EDITOR  ·  A NOTE ON METHOD

Every June a thousand scouts, executives, and coaches convene to guess which twenty-year-olds will become professional basketball players. They watch tape, they read measurements, they argue about motor. This study asks a narrower and more tractable question: given only what a prospect did in college — and what he was thought of in high school — how much of his four-year NBA value can be recovered by a machine?

The answer, for now, is modest. A gradient-boosted tree explains about a fifth of the variance in four-year VORP out of sample. The remaining four-fifths is the reason teams still hire scouts. What follows is an accounting of what the model saw, where it agreed with history, and where it dissents.

What the clusters actually are.

The unsupervised k-means doesn't know anything about NBA outcomes; it only sees the college-era features. At this resolution it carves the draft pool into a handful of recognizable basketball types, each with its own mean four-year value. Archetype names are derived on the fly from whichever stats most distinguish the cluster.
